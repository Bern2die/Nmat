1.1 Subatomic Particles

Electrons are present in the space around the nucleus of an atom and is associated with varying levels of energy.

They contain a negative charge in equal magnitude to the proton denoted as -1e or simply -e.

The mass of an electron is roughly 2000 times smaller than a proton.

Because of this unlike charge it has in relation to a proton, the existing electrostatic attraction outweighs the gravitational attraction based on their respective weights. This makes the electron orbit around an atom's nucleus.

Electrons exist in varying distances away from the nucleus and it's this distance that determines the reactivity of that element. The further away an electron is, the higher its energy levels are. Electrons existing closer to the nucleus have a lower energy level.

The most reactive electron is the one found in the outermost shell and is referred to as the valence electron. This electron is more reactive with the outside environment.

Valence electrons are much more likely to be involved with bonds as they experience the least attraction from the nucleus. Generally, the valence electron determines the reactivity of the atom.

In a neutral state, the atom has an equal # of protons and electrons but if an electron is taken away then the atom gains a positive charge and is called an cation. But if an electron is gained, it gains a negative charge and is called an anion
