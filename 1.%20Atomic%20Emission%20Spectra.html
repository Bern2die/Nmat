At room temperature, atoms will typically be in a ground state but when some form of energy (heat or whatever), the atom can be changed to an excitable state. This excitable state lasts only for a short period of time before returning back to the ground state. But during the time it's in an excited state, it releases a discrete amounts of energy in the forms of photons.

Electrons in an atom can be excited to different energy levels. When they are excited they will promptly go back to a grounded state and when this happens, it will emit photons with a wavelength specific to the energy required for the transition (emits a specific colour). The values that result from this are not in a continuum but rather they are quantized to specific discrete values. Thus, the spectrum is composed of light specified at certain frequencies.

The electron absorbs energy when it is just the correct amount and colour and becomes excited and emits that energy again when it returns back to a ground state. The absorption and emission is conservation of energy and when that happens, photons are emitted with wavelengths in visible light. This results in displaying a specific range of colours unique to the element.

This spectrum is sometimes called the line spectrum, where each line corresponds to a specific electron transition. Since each element can have its electrons be put into a specific excited state, each atom possesses a unique atomic emission spectrum. This unique characteristic can be used much like a fingerprint identity. It produces a specific range of colours depending on the element that specifies its identity.

Application: Physical analysis of the stars and planets may not be feasible but we can analyse the light emitting from a star in its component wavelengths. The wavelengths can then be matched to known line spectra of elements, and thus determining what kind of elements can be found.

Real World: Fluorescence results from the emissions of electrons of an atom dropping from an excited state to a ground state. The emissions produce the light.

Atomic Emission Spectrum of Hydrogen

There are different series' to describe the emission lines corresponding to the transitions in energy levels in the hydrogen atom.

When an electron from a higher energy state (n >= 2) transitions into the lowest orbital (n=1) the series of spectral lines is known as the Lyman series. The values obtained from this series lie in the UV region of the spectra.

When an electron falls from a higher energy state to the 2nd orbital (n = 2), then the series is known as the Balmer series. The values obtained from this series also lies in the UV reigon.

However, the Lyman series includes larger energy transitions compared to the Balmer series, thus it has shorter photon wavelengths in the UV region of the electromagnetic spectrum.

When an electron fall from a higher energy state to the 3rd orbital (n = 3) then the series is known as the Paschen Series and its values lies in the infrared region of the electromagnetic spectrum.

If an electron goes to the 4th orbital (n = 4), the Brackket series is obtained.

If an electron falls to the 5th orbital (n = 5), the Pfund series is obtained.
