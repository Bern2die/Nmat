1.2 Atomic Mass vs. Atomic Weight

Atomic Weight

In nature, elements occur in two or more forms of their isotopes, which are also present in similar proportions in a given sample of that naturally occurring element.

The weighted average of the most abundant isotopes of a given element make up the atomic weight, which is also the number displayed on the periodic table.

ex. Chlorine-35 and Cl-37 show up most abundantly in nature but Cl-35 is about 3 times more abundant. Therefore, the atomic weight of Cl is closer to 35. As it appears on the periodic table, it is listed as approx 35.5

Avodgadro's Number

It should be noted that with an element that has two or more isotopes, its atomic weight will not represent any of one of those isotopes as it is an average between all isotopes of that element.

The atomic weight just helps to quantify and represent the average mass of the element, in amu and the mass of one mole of the element.

A mole is a number of things that equal to Avogadro's number,

NA = 6.02 x 1023

ex. For Carbon, it has 12 grams/mole.

This means that it weighs at 12 amu (being the most abundant isotope)

And it also means that 6.02 x 1023 number of carbon atoms equals 12 grams

How does the half-life help determine the relative proportions of different isotopes?

Half-life corresponds with stability. The more stable isotopes are more abundant as they last longer
