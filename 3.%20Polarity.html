1. Properties of Covalent Compounds

Polarity occurs when two atoms have a relative difference in electronegativities.

When these atoms come together in covalent bonds, they must negotiate the degree to which the electron pairs will be shared.

The atom with the higher electronegativity gets the larger share of the electron density.

A polar bond creates a dipole, with the positive end of the dipole at the less electronegative atom and the negative end at the more electronegative atom.

When atoms that have identical or nearly identical electronegativities share electron pairs, they do so with equal distribution of the electrons. This is called a nonpolar covalent bond, and there is no separation of charge across the bond.

Note that only bonds between atoms of the same element will have exactly the same electronegativity and therefore exhibit a purely equal distribution of electrons.

The seven common diatomic molecules are H2, N2, O2, F2, Cl2, Br2, and I2. At the same time, many bonds are close to nonpolar. Any bond between atoms with a difference in electronegativity less than 0.5 is generally considered nonpolar.
