1.3 Rutherford, Planck, and Bohr

Neils Bohr built upon the work of Planck and Rutherford to develop his model of the atom.

Based of Rutherford's findings, Bohr assumed that the hydrogen atom had a central proton that was orbited by an electron. He postulated that the force responsible for the orbit was from an electrostatic force created due to the unlike charges of the proton and electron.

Bohr then used Planck's quantum theory to correct assumptions made based on classical mechanics. These assumptions made postulated that the electron can assume infinite orbit values (radius and velocity) around a nucleus, therefore the angular momentum (L = mvr) and kinetic energy (k = 1/2[mv2]) could take any value.

Structure of Hydrogen

Bohr described the structure of hydrogen to consist of a dense nucleus that had one proton with electrons orbiting around in a defined pathway (orbit) at discrete energy levels. If enough energy was added to bridge the gap between the orbitals, the electron can jump from a lower-energy orbital to a higher one. These higher-energy orbitals had a larger radii.

At the lowest possible energy level, the atom is defined to be in a ground state (n = 1) where the orbit is the smallest, and therefore had the lowest energy. For atoms described to be in a ground state, it would be in a state of lowest energy, where electrons are placed in the lowest possible orbitals.

In the case where an electron can be found in a higher than normal orbital (higher energy), the atom is said to be in an excited state.

Bohr's model was updated to explain that, rather than electrons residing around orbitals, it was more like electrons could be found in specific regions around an electron rather than having specific pathways.
